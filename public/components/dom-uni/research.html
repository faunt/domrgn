§1 Clue is in the title

“Good fences make good neighbours”

But do they? I take this to be the central question of Frost’s poem. Protagonist hopes to dissuade his neighbour from this isolationist mantra. Is there a clue in how to approach design solution in light of this irony?

  • consider apps as fences, not just bridges but barriers to human connectness
  • overcome barriers through collective effort? Hence mending a common good as in “Mending Wall”
  • apps don’t reach everyone, miss most people. What solution does Frost give?
  • walls not for keeping things out but keeping things in. Can app proposition help people keep private things private

§2

I wrote a paper on this at university

Summary of relevant ideas from Educating the Community paper:

  • more than opt-in “good Samaritan”: provoke debate to improve understanding and respect for diverse community attitudes
  • foster respect for ‘neutrality’
  • confront rather than efface clashing goals and projects(pp 11)
  • challenge community and respect the conflicting interests of others
  • need something to engage about

Aim here to demonstrate capacity for philosophical rigor, for finding what’s at stake

“I simply didn’t know how to get this conversation off the ground. Maybe debate is only appropriate where conflicts arise, as a way of addressing social problems (like market failures, for instance) that have surfaced naturally. You can’t have worthwhile conversation when nothing seems to be at stake.”

— from my paper Educating the Community: Market Failures and Better Neighbours, UofT 2007 (pp 13)

§4 Competitor review

Abundance of existing platforms well positioned to handle hyperlocal.

Facebook and Twitter already platforms of record w/r/t broad user base, likely already reaching your neighbour. To some extent, already connecting around local issues: dog attack in #thispark, geotagging content to neighbourhood level, etc.

Canonical empty room or cold start problem. Combat with targeted rollouts, seed platform with community events?

e.g. voXup.co.uk rallies community to issues, sell to local council (~30 voters = relevance threshold)

While Neighbourhood Watch brought communities together around a common goal — or shared enemy — seems to have failed at fostering tolerance for the neighbour. All about othering.
